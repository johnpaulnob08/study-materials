The Classical Ontological Argument

Anselm, Proslogion — Chapters II–V
Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109)

Christian philosopher, theologian, and Archbishop of Canterbury. Best known for his Proslogium, where he presents a single argument for God's existence requiring no proof beyond itself. The term "ontological argument" was coined later by Immanuel Kant (from Greek ontos = being, logos = rational account).

What Is the Ontological Argument?

Unlike the cosmological or design arguments — which start from things we observe in the world — the ontological argument starts entirely from concepts and definitions. It tries to show that just by understanding what "God" means, you are logically forced to conclude that God exists.

In short: God's existence can be proven from the concept of God alone.

Anselm's Core Idea: "That Than Which Nothing Greater Can Be Conceived"

Anselm defines God as "a being than which nothing greater can be conceived" — the absolutely greatest possible being. Even a fool (atheist) who denies God's existence understands this phrase when they hear it. When you understand it, the concept is in your mind.

The Core Argument (Chapter II, Simplified)
  1. God is "a being than which nothing greater can be conceived." (By definition.)
  2. This being exists, at minimum, in our understanding (in the mind).
  3. A being that exists both in the mind and in reality is greater than a being that exists only in the mind.
  4. If God existed only in the mind, we could conceive of an even greater being — one that also exists in reality. But that would contradict step 1 (God is already the greatest conceivable).
  5. Conclusion: Therefore, God must exist in reality, not just in the mind.
Anselm's Painter Analogy

Before a painter creates a painting, the painting exists in his mind but not yet in reality. After he paints it, it exists both in his mind and in reality. The completed painting is "more real" than the mere mental idea. Anselm uses this to show that there is a difference between existing only in the mind and existing in reality — and existence in reality is "greater."

Chapter III: God Cannot Be Conceived Not to Exist

Anselm goes further: not only does God exist, but God necessarily exists — meaning it is impossible to even coherently imagine God not existing.

Necessary Existence Argument

A being that cannot be conceived not to exist is greater than a being that can be conceived not to exist. Since God is the greatest conceivable being, God cannot be conceived as non-existent. Therefore, God's existence is necessary — He cannot not exist.

Chapter IV: How the Fool Can "Say" God Doesn't Exist

Anselm addresses the obvious objection: "But people do deny God exists!" He distinguishes two senses of "conceiving" something:

  • Saying a word without understanding it — someone can utter "God does not exist" while not really grasping what "God" means. In this word-only sense, yes, someone can say God doesn't exist.
  • Truly understanding the thing itself — once a person truly understands what "God" means (the greatest conceivable being), they cannot coherently think that being does not exist.

The fool, therefore, only "says" God doesn't exist in the shallow sense — using words without proper understanding.

Key Vocabulary

TermMeaning
OntologicalFrom Greek ontos (being). Relating to the nature of existence itself.
"Being than which nothing greater can be conceived"Anselm's definition of God — the perfect, maximum being.
Exists "in the understanding"The concept is in your mind, whether real or not (like a unicorn).
Exists "in reality"Actually exists outside the mind in the world.
Necessary existenceCannot possibly not exist; non-existence is logically impossible.
Famous Objection (Gaunilo's Island)

A monk named Gaunilo objected: by Anselm's logic, we could prove the existence of a "perfect island" — just define it as "the island than which none greater can be conceived" and it must exist! Anselm replied that the argument only works for beings that possess necessary existence by nature — islands are contingent things, not the kind of being that cannot fail to exist.

Bottom Line

Anselm's ontological argument is a purely a priori (reasoning alone, no experience needed) proof for God. The logic: if God is the greatest conceivable being, and existence in reality is greater than existence only in the mind, then God must exist in reality. To deny God's existence is to contradict yourself — you'd be saying the greatest being lacks something (real existence).

The Classical Cosmological Argument

Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologiae
Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)

Dominican monk, theologian, and philosopher; considered the Catholic Church's greatest theologian. His "Five Ways" (Quinque Viae) are five distinct proofs for God's existence drawn from observable facts about the world. The first four are cosmological arguments; the fifth is teleological. From his Summa Theologiae.

What Is the Cosmological Argument?

Unlike the ontological argument (which is purely conceptual), the cosmological argument begins with observable facts about the universe — things exist and change — and reasons backward to a first cause. The key word is "cosmos" (the universe), because the argument starts from the universe's existence and character.

The First Way: Argument from Motion

Step by Step
  1. We observe things in motion (change) around us. Fire heats wood; wood doesn't heat itself.
  2. Whatever is in motion must be put in motion by something else. A thing cannot be both the mover and the moved simultaneously in the same respect.
  3. An infinite regress of movers is impossible — there would then be no first mover, and without a first, there would be no subsequent movers, and nothing would be in motion at all.
  4. Therefore, there must be a First Mover, itself unmoved. This is what everyone understands to be God.
Key Analogy: Potency and Act

Fire (in a state of "act" or actuality) makes wood hot (brings it from "potency" to "act"). Something can only change if something else actualizes its potential. Hot things cannot simultaneously be cold; a mover and a moved cannot be identical in the same respect.

The Second Way: Argument from Efficient Causation

Step by Step
  1. We observe a chain of efficient causes (causes that produce effects) in the world.
  2. Nothing can be its own efficient cause — it would have to exist before itself, which is impossible.
  3. A chain of causes cannot go back infinitely (same logic: no first cause = no subsequent causes = no effects now).
  4. Therefore, there must be a First Efficient Cause — uncaused. This is God.

The Third Way: Argument from Possibility and Necessity

This is the most philosophically sophisticated of the five.

Contingent Being

A being that can exist or not exist — it is generated, corrupts, dies. Most things in nature are contingent.

Necessary Being

A being that must exist — it cannot fail to exist. It has its necessity from itself, not from another.

Step by Step
  1. Everything in nature is contingent — it can fail to exist.
  2. If everything were contingent, there would have been a time when nothing existed at all.
  3. But if nothing existed, nothing could have begun to exist (something must exist to bring other things into being).
  4. Yet things clearly exist now. Contradiction.
  5. Therefore, at least one Necessary Being must exist — that which cannot not be. This is God.

The Fourth Way: Argument from Gradation

Step by Step
  1. We observe degrees of perfection in the world — some things are more good, more true, more noble than others.
  2. Things are said to be "more or less" something according to how closely they resemble a maximum (fire is the hottest; it causes all heat in other things).
  3. Therefore, there must be a maximum being — most good, most true, most noble — which is the cause of all goodness and being in others. This is God.

The Fifth Way: Argument from Design (Brief — Developed in Topic 3)

Natural things without knowledge act toward ends as though guided by an intelligent designer (an arrow needs an archer). Therefore an intelligent being directs all natural things to their ends. This is God.

(The Fifth Way is more fully discussed as the Teleological Argument.)

Aquinas's Replies to Objections

Aquinas anticipated two major objections:

  • Objection 1: If God exists and is infinitely good, why is there evil? Aquinas replies (following Augustine): God's infinite goodness means He can bring good out of evil — evil is permitted precisely so that good may arise from it.
  • Objection 2: Can't we explain everything through nature and human reason without God? Aquinas replies: natural causes and human will themselves trace back to higher causes; ultimately all must trace to an immovable, self-necessary first principle.
Bottom Line

All five of Aquinas's Ways share a common structure: they observe something about the universe (motion, causation, contingency, gradation, purpose), argue that an infinite regress is impossible, and conclude that there must be a First (Unmoved Mover / Uncaused Cause / Necessary Being / Greatest Being / Intelligent Director). All roads lead to God.

The Classical Design / Teleological Argument

William Paley, Natural Theology (1802) + Chapter on Teleological Arguments
William Paley (1743–1805)

English theologian, moral philosopher, and Christian apologist in eighteenth-century England. His book Natural Theology (1802) is the classic defense of the design argument. Best known for the Watchmaker Analogy.

What Is the Teleological Argument?

Teleological (from Greek: telos + logos)

Telos = end or goal; logos = reason or rational account. The teleological argument says that the ends/goals we observe in nature are best explained by a purposive designer. Also called the argument from design.

Where the cosmological argument focuses on the existence of the universe, the teleological argument focuses on its properties — particularly the apparent order, purpose, and means-to-ends design found in nature. It concludes not just that a creator exists, but that the creator has mental properties: intention, knowledge, and purpose.

The argument can be traced back to ancient thinkers (Heraclitus, Plato, Aristotle, the Stoics; in India, the Nyāya school ca. 100–1000 CE), but found its most influential expression in Paley.

Paley's Watchmaker Analogy — The Central Argument

The Famous Analogy (Opening of Natural Theology)

Suppose you were crossing a heath and found a stone. You might say it had always been there — no big deal. But suppose you found a watch. When you inspect it, you see that its parts are perfectly fitted together for a purpose: springs, gears, and a glass face all work together to tell the time. You would conclude without hesitation that the watch had a maker — someone who designed it with intelligence and purpose. The watch could not have arisen by chance.

The analogy: Nature is like a watch — only far more complex and intricate. When we look at the human eye, a bird's wing, or a complex organism, we find the same kind of means-to-ends complexity. Therefore, just as the watch implies a watchmaker, nature implies a grand Designer — God.

The Formal Argument
  1. Complex objects whose parts serve a purpose imply a designer.
  2. Nature contains objects of extreme complexity and apparent purpose (far more than any watch).
  3. Therefore, nature implies a designer of great intelligence, power, and purpose.
  4. This designer is God.

Paley's Pre-Emptive Objections — Why the Argument Still Holds

Paley anticipates objections and shows none of them undermine the inference to a designer:

  • I: "We've never seen a watch being made." — Ignorance of how a watch was made does not change the inference. The design is evident regardless of whether we've witnessed its creation.
  • II: "The watch sometimes goes wrong." — Imperfection does not disprove design. The purpose of the machinery is still evident; we don't need perfection to recognize intention.
  • III: "Some parts seem unnecessary." — Having some parts we don't understand doesn't undermine our understanding of the parts we do. The overall evidence for design remains.
  • IV: "Maybe it arose from chance combinations." — No sensible person would believe the watch's complex configuration just happened by chance assembly of materials.
  • V: "Maybe there's just a principle of order in nature." — You can't separate "order" from an agent who orders. Order requires an orderer; you can't substitute an abstract principle for intelligent agency.
  • VI–VIII: "It's just the laws of metallic/natural nature at work." — Laws describe how agents act; they do not replace agents. A law "implies a power; for it is only the mode, according to which an agent proceeds." Without the agent, the law does nothing.

The Teleological Argument More Broadly

The textbook chapter extends beyond Paley to note:

  • Teleological arguments begin with specific properties of the world (order, purpose, complexity) rather than mere existence.
  • They conclude with a designer having mental properties — intention, knowledge, purpose — not just a "first cause."
  • Cosmological arguments → First Cause; Teleological arguments → Intelligent Designer.
Classic Objection: David Hume & Charles Darwin

David Hume (before Darwin) argued the analogy between a watch and nature is weak — they are too dissimilar to support the same conclusion. Charles Darwin's theory of evolution later offered an alternative explanation for biological complexity: natural selection, not a designer. Most modern versions of the teleological argument address these challenges.

Bottom Line

Paley's design argument: the complex, purposive structure of nature — especially living organisms — cannot be explained by chance. Just as a watch implies a watchmaker, nature implies God as a grand intelligent designer. Key insight: Paley shows that not knowing exactly how the watch was made, or finding some imperfection in it, does not weaken the inference to a maker at all.

The Problem of Evil

Pojman, Philosophy of Religion — Chapter on The Problem of Evil

What Is the Problem of Evil?

The problem of evil is the most powerful objection against the existence of God. It's used by atheologians (those who argue against God's existence) to claim that belief in the Judeo-Christian God is unreasonable or even internally contradictory.

The Judeo-Christian tradition affirms four propositions simultaneously:

  1. God is all-powerful (omnipotent).
  2. God is all-knowing (omniscient).
  3. God is perfectly good (omnibenevolent).
  4. Evil exists in the world.

The problem: if God is perfectly good, He would not want evil; if He is omniscient, He knows about it; if He is omnipotent, He can prevent it. So why does evil exist at all? It seems that God and evil cannot coexist.

Two Types of Evil

TypeDefinitionExamples
Moral Evil Evil for which creatures (humans) are morally responsible Murder, theft, war, cruelty, deception
Natural Evil (also: surd evil) Terrible events that arise from nature itself, without human agency Earthquakes, hurricanes, natural diseases, floods, volcanic eruptions

Note: Some theists argue all evil is essentially moral, claiming the devil is responsible even for natural evil.

The Main Theistic Defense: The Free Will Defense

The most important theistic response, traceable to St. Augustine (354–430 CE) and developed by philosophers like John Hick, Alvin Plantinga, and Richard Swinburne.

The Free Will Defense

God gave human beings genuine free will — the capacity to choose between good and evil. A world with free creatures who can genuinely choose good is more valuable than a world of morally perfect robots who are programmed to do good. But free will entails the possibility of choosing evil. God could have prevented all evil only by taking away human freedom — and that would be a greater loss than the evil it prevents.

Therefore, moral evil is explained by human free choice. God is not responsible for what free creatures freely choose.

Limits of the Free Will Defense

The free will defense handles moral evil well — but what about natural evil (earthquakes, diseases)? Human free will does not cause earthquakes. So the defense alone doesn't resolve the full problem of evil. Some theists extend it by attributing natural evil to the devil, or argue that a world with natural challenges develops virtuous souls (the "soul-making" theodicy of John Hick).

The Epicurean Challenge

Epicurus's Famous Trilemma (341–270 BCE)

"Is he willing to prevent evil, but not able? Then he is impotent. Is he able, but not willing? Then he is malevolent. Is he both able and willing? Whence then is evil?"

This three-pronged challenge captures the logical tension at the heart of the problem. If God can prevent evil and chooses not to, He seems malevolent. If He wants to but can't, He's not omnipotent. Either way, the traditional God seems impossible.

Key Vocabulary

TermMeaning
OmnipotentAll-powerful; able to do anything
OmniscientAll-knowing; knows all past, present, future
OmnibenevolentPerfectly good; wills only what is good
AtheologianOne who argues against the existence of God
TheodicyA defense or justification of God's goodness in light of evil
Free Will DefenseGod allows evil because freedom is more valuable than a world without evil
Surd evilAnother name for natural evil — evil that seems pointless or purposeless
Bottom Line

The problem of evil: if God is all-powerful, all-knowing, and all-good, why does evil exist? The best theistic response is the Free Will Defense — God gave humans freedom, and evil results from that freedom. However, this mostly addresses moral evil; natural evil remains more difficult to explain. The problem of evil is the strongest argument against God's existence.

The Idea of the Holy

Rudolf Otto, The Idea of the Holy
Rudolf Otto (1869–1937)

German Protestant theologian and philosopher of religion. The Idea of the Holy (German: Das Heilige, 1917) is his most influential work. Otto argued that religion cannot be reduced to rational concepts or moral obligations — there is a fundamentally non-rational core to religious experience which he called the numinous.

The Rational vs. the Non-Rational in Religion

Theology traditionally describes God using rational attributes: Spirit, Reason, Purpose, Good Will, Supreme Power, Unity, Selfhood. These are rational attributes — they can be grasped by the intellect and defined conceptually. Otto does not deny their importance.

But his central claim is: these rational attributes do NOT exhaust the idea of God. They imply a deeper subject — something that cannot be fully captured in concepts. This deeper, irreducible element is the non-rational core of religious experience.

Rationalism (Otto's sense)

The view that the essence of deity can be completely and exhaustively expressed in rational concepts. Otto criticizes this as one-sided and misleading.

Otto's argument: Rationalism — even in orthodox Christianity — has overstressed the rational side and failed to do justice to the non-rational. The result is a "one-sidedly intellectualistic and rationalistic" idea of God.

The Concept of "Holiness" — And Its Problem

In everyday use, "holy" has come to mean simply "morally perfect" (e.g., Kant called the perfectly dutiful will a "holy will"). But Otto argues this is inaccurate — the original Hebrew qadosh, Greek hagios, and Latin sacer meant something other than moral goodness. They pointed to a specific, unique quality that is not the same as moral goodness — an extra dimension beyond the ethical.

To isolate this extra element, Otto coins a new word:

The Numinous

From Latin numen (divine presence/power). The "numinous" is that unique, non-rational, non-moral element in the idea of the holy — the extra quality beyond "perfectly good" that cannot be fully defined, only evoked. It is the mysterious, awe-inspiring core of religious experience. Every religion has it at its core.

Creature-Feeling: The Human Response to the Numinous

When one encounters the numinous, the characteristic response is what Otto calls creature-feeling or creature-consciousness: an overwhelming awareness of one's own smallness, nothingness, and abasement before the awesome, overpowering presence of something supremely "other."

Abraham's Example (Genesis 18:27)

"Behold now, I have taken upon me to speak unto the Lord, which am but dust and ashes." This is creature-feeling in its purest form — the sense of being utterly nothing before the divine majesty.

Otto distinguishes his creature-feeling from Schleiermacher's "feeling of dependence." Schleiermacher described religion as a "feeling of absolute dependence." Otto agrees this is close but criticizes it as not quite right: the creature-feeling is not primarily about the self (self-depreciation), but is a response to something outside the self — the numinous object. The self-abasement is secondary; the presence of the numinous comes first.

Mysterium Tremendum — The Heart of the Numinous

The numinous experience can best be captured by the Latin phrase mysterium tremendum — literally: "awe-inspiring mystery." It has three elements:

1. The Mysterium (Mystery)

The numinous object is wholly other — utterly unlike anything in the natural world. It is hidden, beyond comprehension, extraordinary, and unfamiliar. But "mystery" here doesn't just mean "unknown" — it refers to something that is positively other and beyond, not just conceptually undefined. It is encountered, not merely absent.

2. The Tremendum (Awe/Dread)

This is not ordinary fear, but a specific kind of dread — a holy terror. Otto traces three sub-elements:

  • Awfulness — a shuddering, creeping sensation. "My flesh crept." "My blood ran cold." A specific physiological response to something uncanny and unearthly. Its primitive form is "daemonic dread"; its refined form is holy awe.
  • Overpoweringness (Majestas) — the sense of an absolute, overwhelming might before which the self is nothing. This generates the creature-feeling. The difference between feeling "caused" (Schleiermacher) and feeling crushed by supreme majesty.
  • Energy / Urgency — the numinous has a quality of vitality, passion, force, and will. In the Hebrew Bible, this appears as the orge (wrath) of Yahweh — not moral anger, but an uncanny, incalculable, "stored-up electricity" quality.

3. The Fascinans (Fascination)

Despite being terrifying, the numinous is also strangely attractive — it draws us in. The response is not pure terror but a paradox of dread and fascination, repulsion and attraction. This "fascination" is the positive, captivating pull of the holy.

The Full Numinous Experience

The complete numinous experience combines mysterium + tremendum + fascinans: it is something wholly other, awesome and terrifying, yet also strangely compelling and attractive. Worship, awe, mystical ecstasy — all are expressions of this complex response.

Key Vocabulary

TermMeaning
NuminousThe non-rational, non-moral quality of the holy; the divine presence that evokes awe
Mysterium tremendum"Awe-inspiring mystery" — the core phrase capturing the numinous experience
Creature-feelingThe sense of absolute nothingness and smallness before the divine
MajestasAbsolute overpoweringness; the overwhelming might of the divine
Orge (Wrath of Yahweh)The energetic, dynamic, "electric" quality of the divine — not moral anger but numinous energy
Wholly other (ganz andere)The numinous is entirely unlike anything in ordinary experience
FascinansThe fascinating, attractive side of the holy — what draws us toward it despite the dread
SchematizationThe process by which moral and rational content fills in the originally non-rational concept of "holy"
Bottom Line

Otto argues that religion has a non-rational core that cannot be captured in rational concepts or moral categories alone. This core is the numinous — the awe-inspiring mystery of the divine. The human response to it is the mysterium tremendum: a combination of holy dread, creature-feeling (self-abasement), and fascination. All religions share this numinous element at their heart. The mistake of Rationalism is to reduce God entirely to moral and intellectual concepts, losing this living, experiential core.

The Sacred and the Profane

Mircea Eliade, The Sacred and the Profane
Mircea Eliade (1907–1986)

Romanian-born scholar and leading figure in religious studies and comparative religion. Born in Bucharest, he became a professor at the University of Chicago and is known for his work on religious symbolism, mythology, and the sacred. His concept of the "sacred and the profane" is foundational to the study of religion.

The Core Distinction: Sacred vs. Profane

Eliade argues that the most fundamental distinction in religious experience is between the sacred and the profane. These are two modes of being — two fundamentally different ways of experiencing the world.

The Sacred

The sacred is that which is set apart from ordinary existence — a space, time, object, or person imbued with a special significance, power, and reality. It is where the transcendent is believed to manifest uniquely. The sacred is experienced as fundamentally different in kind from the profane — not just more important, but of a different order of reality altogether.

The Profane

The ordinary, everyday world — lacking special sacred significance or power. The secular world of routines, daily tasks, and homogenous space and time. The profane is not evil — it is simply ordinary.

Sacred Space

A sacred space is a place set apart from the ordinary world where the transcendent manifests in a unique way. Sacred spaces are distinct, special environments where religious rituals, ceremonies, and encounters with the divine occur.

Key characteristics of sacred space:

  • Set Apart: Distinctly separated from the ordinary profane world. There are boundaries — thresholds, doors, altars — marking the transition from profane to sacred.
  • Transcendence: Sacred spaces are believed to be places where the transcendent (divine) is specially present or accessible.
  • Power: Sacred spaces carry a special force, energy, or significance not found in ordinary spaces.
  • Orientation: In Eliade's view, sacred space provides a "center of the world" (axis mundi) — a fixed point of orientation around which all other space is organized. It creates order out of chaos.

Examples of sacred spaces: Temples, shrines, churches, cathedrals, mountains, hills (like Mt. Sinai), ceremonial circles, pilgrimage sites (like Lourdes, Mecca).

Profane Space

Profane space is the ordinary, everyday environment — homogeneous (all parts are equal), undifferentiated, lacking in inherent spiritual quality.

Examples of profane spaces: Homes and dwellings (in their everyday function), marketplaces, streets and public spaces, workplaces and offices.

The Hierophany — The Appearance of the Sacred

Hierophany

From Greek hieros (sacred) + phainein (to appear). A hierophany is any act or moment in which the sacred manifests itself — where the sacred "shows itself" in something. A burning bush, a sacred mountain, a holy relic, a consecrated object — all are hierophanies. They are moments when the transcendent breaks into ordinary, profane space.

When a hierophany occurs, the ordinary object or place is transformed — it becomes sacred. But it doesn't cease to be itself (a stone remains a stone, a tree remains a tree); it simply now also participates in the sacred.

Sacred Time

Eliade extends the sacred/profane distinction from space to time. Sacred time is not ordinary, sequential, historical time — it is mythical time, the "Great Time" of origins, when the sacred events happened. Religious rituals allow humans to re-enter sacred time — to reactualize the primordial events (creation, divine deeds) and participate in the sacred reality once more.

Profane time is ordinary linear time — one thing after another, with no special significance.

The Axis Mundi — Center of the World

Axis Mundi

The "world axis" or "cosmic axis" — the point where heaven, earth, and the underworld meet. Every sacred space functions as an axis mundi: the center of the world, the point of contact between cosmic realms. Sacred mountains, temples, altars, and poles in many cultures serve as axis mundi. It is around this center that the world becomes ordered and livable.

Comparing Sacred and Profane

SacredProfane
NatureSet apart, extraordinary, transcendentOrdinary, everyday, secular
SpaceNon-homogeneous; has a "center" (axis mundi)Homogeneous; all parts equivalent
TimeCyclical, mythical; re-entry into originsLinear, historical, profane duration
ExperienceAwe, reverence, tremblingRoutine, mundane, ordinary
ExamplesTemple, mountain, shrine, ritualMarketplace, office, street
Bottom Line

Eliade argues that all religious experience is organized around the fundamental distinction between the sacred and the profane. The sacred is that which is set apart, extraordinary, and transcendent; the profane is the ordinary world. Sacred spaces (temples, mountains, shrines) function as axis mundi — centers where heaven and earth meet. The sacred "breaks in" to the profane through hierophanies. Religion is, at its core, the human experience of and response to the sacred.

Miracles and the Modern Worldview

Chapter 8 — Philosophy of Religion

The Central Question

The chapter asks: Is it still possible to believe in miracles? And if miracles can occur, is it ever reasonable to believe one has occurred? Two major thinkers anchor the discussion: Rudolf Bultmann (arguing it's no longer possible) and David Hume (arguing it's never reasonable).

Miracle (Popular Sense)

An unexpected, beneficial, extraordinary event — "It's a miracle I passed that exam!" This is the loose, everyday meaning.

Miracle (Strict/Philosophical Sense)

An event that (1) would not have occurred had only natural causes been at work, AND (2) was brought about by God or a supernatural agent. Both conditions must be satisfied.

Bultmann: Miracles Are Incompatible with the Modern World View

Rudolf Bultmann (1884–1976)

German biblical historian and theologian. Argued that belief in miracles belongs to a pre-scientific worldview that modern people can no longer hold.

Bultmann's claim: The modern scientific worldview treats nature as a closed, autonomous realm — every event in nature is accounted for by another event in nature. Science and technology have given us this worldview; it is impossible for modern people to believe in supernatural interventions.

Bultmann's Famous Statement

"It is impossible to use electric light and the wireless and to avail ourselves of modern medical and surgical discoveries, and at the same time to believe in the New Testament world of spirits and miracles."

The modern world view means: when a TV breaks, we look for electrical failure — not demons. When disease strikes, we look for germs and viruses — not God's punishment. There is simply no room for supernatural explanation in the modern mindset.

Criticism of Bultmann

The author argues Bultmann's claim is too strong. People today do still believe in miracles, so it is clearly possible to do so. Also, Bultmann commits a logical error: the fact that science makes natural explanations our first resort does not commit us to rejecting all supernatural explanations. The modern scientific worldview makes miracles harder to accept, but not logically impossible.

Hume's Definition of Miracles

Hume's Definition (Strict Sense)

A miracle is "a transgression of a law of nature by a particular volition of the Deity, or by the interposition of some invisible agent." Two necessary conditions: (1) the event would not have occurred by natural causes alone; (2) it is caused by God or a supernatural agent.

Hume also identifies objections to his definition:

  • Some argue that miracles must also be astounding/surprising (Condition 3) and beneficial (Condition 4). Hume disagrees — these are conditions for us to recognize a miracle, not conditions for it to be a miracle. A small invisible miracle (a leaf moved an extra inch by God) would still be a miracle even if no one noticed.

Hume's Central Argument Against Believing in Miracles

Hume's famous argument: it is never reasonable to believe a miracle has occurred. Here is the logic:

Hume's Argument
  1. A law of nature is established by uniform, constant experience — the strongest kind of evidence available.
  2. A miracle is, by definition, a violation of a law of nature.
  3. Therefore, the evidence against a miracle (all our past experience supporting the law) is always extremely strong.
  4. The only evidence for a miracle is the testimony of witnesses.
  5. It is always more reasonable to believe witnesses were mistaken or lying than to believe a law of nature was violated.
  6. Conclusion: It is never reasonable to believe a miracle has occurred.

Hume does allow one theoretical exception: if the testimony for an event were so overwhelming and cross-verified that its falsehood would itself be "more miraculous" than the event, we could believe it. But in practice, no miracle testimony is ever that strong.

Two Major Weaknesses in Hume's Argument

Weakness 1: Hume Ignores Indirect Evidence

Hume says the only evidence for a miracle is direct witness testimony. But this is wrong. Evidence can be:

  • Direct evidence: Eyewitness testimony ("I saw a bear in my camp").
  • Indirect evidence: Facts best explained by a hypothesis ("The ice chest is damaged, food is gone, camp is in disarray" — all indirect evidence a bear was there).

Indirect evidence for miracles can be very strong — sometimes stronger than direct evidence. Hume ignored indirect evidence, making his argument incomplete.

The Resurrection Example

The direct testimony of Resurrection eyewitnesses may be weak, but the indirect evidence is compelling: (a) the disciples were deeply depressed after the crucifixion; (b) they had little faith; (c) yet something transformed them into bold believers. The best explanation? Their Master had risen. Indirect evidence here is far stronger than direct testimony.

Weakness 2: Hume Overweights Past Experience

Hume's second error: he gives too much weight to the uniformity of past experience as evidence against an event. In science, exceptions to established laws do occur — and when they do, scientists don't simply dismiss them as impossible. They revise the law. Similarly, when an extraordinary event occurs, the reasonable response is to investigate, not automatically disbelieve.

A Violation of Nature vs. Refuting a Law of Nature

An important distinction: when something unusual happens, there are three possibilities:

  • A natural force is operating that we don't know about (the unusual event conforms to a revised natural law).
  • The unusual event refutes the supposed law (it was never a law of nature).
  • The unusual event truly violates a law of nature (a genuine miracle).

Determining which category an event falls into requires investigation, not automatic dismissal.

When Is It Reasonable to Believe in Divine Intervention?

The chapter concludes: believing a genuine miracle occurred requires two steps:

  1. First, reasonable grounds that a violation of natural law has occurred (not just an unusual event).
  2. Second, reasonable grounds that the violation is due to God's activity specifically.

The second step depends partly on whether we already have good reason to believe God exists. If we do, violations of natural law in circumstances where God would plausibly act are good evidence of miracles. Without theistic belief, it is much harder to conclude any particular violation is divine.

Bottom Line

Bultmann: the modern scientific worldview makes it impossible to believe in miracles. (Verdict: too strong — possible but harder.) Hume: it is never reasonable to believe a miracle occurred because the evidence from uniform experience always outweighs witness testimony. (Verdict: flawed — ignores indirect evidence and overweights past experience.) The chapter concludes that believing in miracles is reasonable if: (a) there is evidence of a genuine violation of natural law, and (b) we have good reason to believe in a God who would intervene in the world.

Human Freedom

William Rowe, Philosophy of Religion — Chapter 10
William Rowe (Author)

The chapter discusses the problem of Predestination, Divine Foreknowledge, and Human Freedom — one of the most ancient and difficult problems in philosophical theology. Rowe presents the problem and its major solutions with unusual clarity and personal engagement.

Two Concepts of Human Freedom

The entire problem turns on which concept of freedom we adopt:

First Idea of Freedom: "Acting Freely = Doing What You Want"

On this view, you act freely when you do what you want or choose to do, without external compulsion. If you want to stay in a room and you stay, you stay freely — even if, unknown to you, the door is locked.

Second Idea of Freedom (More Adequate): "The Power to Do Otherwise"

You act freely only if, at the time just before you do it, it was in your power to do otherwise. Freedom requires genuine alternative possibilities — not just doing what you want, but having the real power to have done differently.

Locke's Locked Room (John Locke, 1632–1704)

A man is brought asleep into a room. The door is locked from outside. He wakes up, wants to stay and talk to friends, so he stays. On the first idea of freedom: he stays freely (he's doing what he wants). But on the second, more adequate idea: he's NOT free — because it was NOT in his power to leave. He would have stayed regardless of his choice. True freedom requires the power to have done otherwise.

Rowe argues the second idea of freedom is more adequate. The first idea would make divine predestination no problem at all — but that seems to evade the real issue rather than resolve it.

The Problem: Divine Foreknowledge vs. Human Freedom

The doctrine of divine foreknowledge says God knows, before we are born, everything we will do. The problem: if God already knew what you would do, how could you have done otherwise?

The Formal Argument
  1. God knows before we are born everything we will do.
  2. If God knows before we are born everything we will do, then it is never in our power to do otherwise.
  3. If it is never in our power to do otherwise, then there is no human freedom.
  4. Therefore: there is no human freedom.

This argument is valid (the logic holds). The question is whether all the premises are true. This limits solutions to four: reject premise 1, 2, or 3, or accept conclusion 4.

Why Premise 2 Is Problematic — The "Closed Past"

The reasoning for premise 2 rests on the fixity of the past: the past cannot be changed. If God knew yesterday that you would do X today, then that knowledge is now a fact about the past. And the past is not in our power to alter. Therefore, you cannot do otherwise than X.

The Classroom Example

It's 2:45 PM Tuesday. You're in class. You now know it was in your power at 2:00 PM to go to a movie instead — you regret not going. But at 2:45, can you make it the case that at 2:30 you went to class? No — the past is fixed. You cannot retroactively change past facts. Now: if before you were born God knew you'd be in class at 2:30, that "F: Before you were born, God knew you'd come to class at 2:30" is a past fact. And past facts are not within our power to alter.

Four Solutions to the Conflict

Solution I: Reject Premise 3 — Redefine Freedom (Jonathan Edwards)

Accept the first idea of freedom: acting freely = doing what you want. On this view, there is no conflict between foreknowledge and freedom. God ordained you to want what you want; you do it freely because you want it. Rowe's verdict: Inadequate — because it relies on the weaker first idea of freedom, which doesn't capture real human responsibility.

Solution II: Reject Premise 2 — "Power Over the Past" (William of Ockham, 1285–1349)

Ockham argues that not all facts about the past are truly "simply about the past." Some facts about the past are also implicitly about the future — and those may be within our power to affect.

Key distinction:

  • Facts simply about the past: "In 1941, Japan attacked Pearl Harbor." No one in 1943 could have affected this.
  • Facts not simply about the past: "In 1941, a war begins that lasts four years." This implies a fact about 1944 — which, in 1943, was still somewhat open.

Similarly, "Before you were born, God knew you'd come to class at 2:30" is not simply about the past — it implies something about your future at 2:30. Since it implies a future fact, it might be within your power to affect. Rowe's verdict: The most promising solution — it preserves both divine foreknowledge and human freedom.

Solution III: Reject Premise 1 — Deny Foreknowledge (Radical)

Perhaps God does not have foreknowledge of future free acts. There are two versions:

  • Open future view: Statements about future contingent events are neither true nor false yet. God cannot "know" what is not yet determined. (Associated with Aristotle.)
  • Boethius/Aquinas view (more widely held): God is eternal in the sense of existing outside of time altogether. He does not "foreknow" — He simply knows, because all of time (past, present, future) is simultaneously present to Him as a single eternal "now." God doesn't know your future acts before they happen — He sees them as they happen from His timeless vantage point.
Boethius's Image

Imagine God looking down from a mountaintop at a long road. Travelers at different parts of the road cannot see each other — one cannot see what's at mile 50 from mile 1. But God, above it all, sees the whole road at once. His knowledge of "future" events is really just knowledge of what is eternally present to Him.

Problem with this solution: If God exists outside time, how can He act in time? Creating, forgiving, answering prayers — all these are temporal acts. An atemporal God seems to be unable to act in history, which undermines much of traditional theism.

Solution IV: Accept Conclusion — Deny Human Freedom (No serious theist accepts this)

Bottom Line

The conflict: if God knows before you are born what you will do, you cannot do otherwise — hence no freedom. Two adequate solutions emerge. The Ockham solution (Solution II): facts about God's foreknowledge are not "simply about the past" — they imply future events still within our power to affect. The Boethius/Aquinas solution (Solution III): God is timeless and doesn't literally "foreknow" — He knows all time from an eternal present, so His knowledge doesn't constrain our freedom. Both solutions are tenable; the first rests on God as everlasting; the second on God as timeless but faces problems about divine agency in time.

Life After Death

William Rowe, Philosophy of Religion — Chapter 9

Overview

The chapter covers: (1) different conceptions of immortality, (2) questions about the meaningfulness of personal survival, (3) arguments for immortality, and (4) the case against immortality.

Rowe separates two questions: the conceptual question (Is belief in life after death even meaningful?) and the factual question (Is it true?).

Varieties of Immortality

1. The Homeric Conception (Ancient Greece)

In early Greek religion, the gods were immortal — humans were not, properly speaking. Yet after death, a shadow of the person survived in Hades — a diminished, inferior form of existence. Not truly living, but not completely gone. Homer's Achilles voices this: better to be a living farm hand than king over the dead.

2. The Platonic Conception

Plato identified the real person with the soul — not the body. The body is a "prison house" for the soul. At death, the soul is liberated from the body and achieves its true, eternal existence. The soul is immortal; the life of the soul after death is superior to earthly life. In Phaedo, Socrates argues for the soul's immortality right before his own death.

3. Eastern Conceptions (Hindu/Buddhist)

Hinduism generally holds the soul (atman) undergoes transmigration (reincarnation) — passing from body to body in a "cycle of rebirth" (samsara). Ultimately the soul achieves release (moksha) — absorption into the Universal Soul (Brahman), losing individual identity and consciousness. Individual immortality is ultimately undesirable; the goal is loss of individual self in the divine totality. Buddhism similarly holds that what we call the "self" is not a permanent substance; the cycle of rebirth continues until Nirvana (extinction of the self).

4. Resurrection of the Body (Christian/Jewish/Islamic)

The person is a unity of soul and body. The continued existence of only the soul (disembodied) would not be the full person. True personal survival requires the reuniting of soul with a resurrected body. The resurrected body, according to St. Paul, is neither physical (corporeal) nor perishable — it is spiritual and immortal.

ConceptionWhat SurvivesNature of Afterlife
HomericA shadow/shadeInferior, ghostly existence in Hades
PlatonicSoul (immaterial substance)Superior, eternal; better than earthly life

The Meaningfulness of Immortality — Two Problems

Problem 1: What does it mean to survive as a "person"?

Persons have: actions, intentions, sensations, emotions, thoughts, memories, perceptions, and physical characteristics. If something survives disembodied, can it perform any of these? Actions involve physical movement. Emotions are closely tied to bodily states. Perceptions (seeing, hearing, tasting) require sensory organs. A purely immaterial soul would seem to lack all of these — raising serious doubt about whether "immortal soul" is a meaningful idea of a person.

Problem 2: What makes the survivor the same person?

Even if something immaterial survives, why would it be you specifically? Personal identity through time involves psychological continuity (memories, personality) and/or bodily continuity. A disembodied soul lacks bodily continuity. The Christian resurrection view has an advantage here: bodily resurrection preserves some form of bodily continuity. But the resurrected body is radically different — spiritual and immortal — which creates its own puzzles.

Arguments FOR Immortality (The Case for Survival)

1. The Philosophical Argument (Plato)

The soul is an immaterial substance. Since material things are destroyed by separation of parts, and the soul has no parts (it is simple and indivisible), the soul cannot be destroyed.

The Argument
  1. A thing can be destroyed only by separating its parts.
  2. The soul has no parts (it is an immaterial, simple substance).
  3. Therefore, the soul cannot be destroyed — it is immortal.
Objections

Kant's Objection: Even if the soul has no spatial parts (no "extensive quantity"), it might still have "intensive quantity" — degrees of consciousness, strength, vitality. If these degrees diminish gradually to zero, the soul has been destroyed not by dissolution but by gradual fading of its powers.

Materialist Objection: There is no immaterial soul substance to begin with. The "soul" is just the organized functioning of the brain. When the brain dies, mental life ceases.

Bundle Theory Objection: The soul is not a single enduring substance but a bundle of mental events connected by memory and sequence. The question of survival becomes: will the bundle of mental events associated with this body continue after the body's death? This is far from obvious.

2. The Scientific Argument: Mental Mediumship

Some scientific evidence (of a parapsychological kind) is offered by the phenomenon of mental mediumship: living persons who claim to communicate with the spirits of the dead. Key features:

  • A medium enters a trancelike state; a "control" (a spirit closely associated with the medium) takes over and relays messages from a "communicator" (the spirit of the deceased).
  • Research organizations (the Society for Psychical Research, founded 1882) have tried to study this scientifically under controlled conditions.
  • The most interesting cases are proxy sittings: where the sitter knows neither the medium nor the deceased, and the medium nonetheless provides highly specific, accurate information.
The Vandy Case

Edgar Vandy, an inventor, died under mysterious circumstances. His brothers hired mediums (blind to the circumstances) using proxy sitters. The mediums produced specific, accurate details about how Vandy died (diving board, swimming pool, blow to the head, water) and about his secret invention (a complex printing/lithography machine). C.D. Broad concluded that the concordance between these details was too specific to be mere coincidence — either fraud, ESP (Super-Extrasensory Perception), or genuine survival.

3. The Theological Argument

If the theistic God exists — a God who created persons for fellowship with Himself — then it would be contrary to God's purposes to allow persons to simply perish at bodily death. God's love for His creatures implies He would not allow permanent extinction. Therefore, if theism is true, immortality is very probably true as well.

Note: This is the strongest argument for immortality, but it depends entirely on theism being true first. Its strength equals the strength of the grounds for theism.

The Case AGAINST Immortality

The main scientific argument against immortality, stated by Bertrand Russell:

The Dependence Argument (Bertrand Russell)

All evidence shows that mental life is dependent on bodily life, particularly on the brain. Brain damage → loss of specific mental functions. Brain death → cessation of all mental activity. It is therefore rational to conclude that when the brain ceases to function permanently, mental life ceases as well. The mind is not a separate substance that survives the body — it is a function of the body.

McTaggart's Window Analogy (Rebuttal to Russell)

Imagine a person locked in a room with only one window. Her experience of the outside world entirely depends on that window. If the window is damaged, boarded up, or covered, her experience of the outside is affected. But this dependence does NOT mean she ceases to exist when the window is removed. Similarly: the mind's dependence on the brain while in a body doesn't prove the mind ceases when the body dies — it might be freed from that dependency at death.

Rowe's evaluation: McTaggart's response is clever, but the analogy is imperfect. The connection between mind and brain is far more intimate than a person and a window — it is the mind itself (memories, personality, thought processes) that is tied to the brain, not just the mind's access to information.

Final Assessment

  • The philosophical argument (Platonic) is the weakest — too many objections to the idea of an immaterial soul substance.
  • The scientific argument (mediumship) may provide some evidence, but it is hard to rule out fraud or ESP.
  • The theological argument is the strongest — but depends on theism.
  • The scientific argument against immortality (dependence on brain) is strong — but might rest on a "false analogy" (McTaggart).
  • Overall: Without theism, we have more reason to doubt personal survival than to believe in it. With theism, personal survival becomes quite reasonable.
Bottom Line

Life after death can be conceived in many ways (Homeric shadow, Platonic soul, Hindu reincarnation, Christian resurrection). The key philosophical questions are whether survival is even meaningful (can a disembodied thing be a person?) and whether survival is the same person. Three arguments support immortality: the philosophical (soul is indestructible), the scientific (mediumship evidence), and the theological (God's purpose implies survival). Against: mental life depends on the brain. The strongest positive case rests on theism; the strongest negative case is the brain-dependence argument. Neither case is conclusive.
