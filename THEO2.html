MODULE 01

Economic Life

How the Church sees money, work, and wealth
The Big Idea

The Church teaches that economic activity is not just about making money — it must serve people, follow moral law, and ensure that wealth is shared. The economy is meant to serve humanity, not the other way around.

Core Teaching Even goods that are legitimately owned are still meant to be shared. Private ownership is not an absolute right — it comes with social responsibility.
Biblical Foundation

Man, Poverty & Riches

The Bible consistently reminds us that wealth is a gift, not a guarantee of worth. The poor have a special place in God's heart.

Wealth Exists to Be Shared

From the Old Testament onward, goods are meant for everyone, not just those who own them. Sharing is a biblical command.
7 Basic Principles of Economic Life
  • Justice governs economy. Economic activity must follow moral limits and serve people's real needs — not just maximize output.
  • Everyone has a right to basic goods. Every person and family deserves a sufficient share of the earth's resources.
  • Prosperity = fair distribution, not just total wealth. A rich country that ignores its poor is NOT truly prosperous.
  • Work must be available. Opportunities to work must be provided for those able and willing.
  • No monopoly on economic decisions. Economic development cannot be controlled by a small powerful group or state alone.
  • Fair taxation. Taxes must be based on one's ability to pay — not a flat burden on everyone.
  • Government has a role. The state must participate in regulating economic life for the common good.
Basic Economic Rights (Church Teaching)

🏢 Employment

Right to productive, meaningful work

💰 Just Wages

Fair pay that supports a dignified life

🏠 Adequate Income

Enough to meet basic needs

🤝 Organize & Bargain

Right to form unions and negotiate

🏡 Own Property

Property as protection of freedom

🗳️ Participate

Voice in economic decisions
Catholic Priorities in Economic Order
  • Needs of the poor (always first)
  • Rights of workers (fair treatment, safe conditions)
  • Preservation of the environment
  • Production to meet social needs (not just profit)
Economy & Morality — How Are They Related?

The Church teaches that economic activity and moral law are inseparable. You cannot have a "good economy" that ignores morality.

  • Profit alone is NOT a good indicator of whether a business is functioning well — it must also uphold human dignity.
  • Business owners and managers must go beyond profit — they have responsibilities to workers, community, and society.
  • Wealth and development become immoral when they increase in quantity but worsen human dignity and fairness.
⚖️

Key Distinction

Private property is real and protected — but NOT absolute. In extreme necessity, a person has the right to take from another's surplus for survival. The needs of life come before property rights.
MODULE 02

Political Life

Authority, rights, and the Catholic view of government
What is Politics?

The word "politics" comes from the Greek Πολιτικά — Aristotle's book on "Affairs of the City." It involves social relations with authority, regulating public affairs, and forming/applying policy.

Catholic Social Teaching The Church's body of doctrine on poverty, economics, social organization, and the role of the state — all centered on the dignity of the human person.
Biblical View of Political Authority

📜 Old Testament

Israel first had no king — only God ruled. When they asked for a king, God warned them. Yet a king's authority can still be a gift from God (1 Sam 9:16). The ideal king is wise, just, peaceful, and cares for the poor.

✝️ New Testament

Jesus did not directly oppose authorities. He said "give to Caesar what is Caesar's." He rejected political messianism and taught leaders to be servants of all.

✉️ Early Church

Paul: submit to legitimate authority (it reflects God's order). Pay taxes. Pray for rulers. Peter: be subject to human institutions — but always within God's higher law.
👑

The Ideal Leader (Biblical)

Wise and just (especially to the poor) · Favors good, punishes evil · Treats all equally · God-fearing · Peacemaker
Political Authority — 4 Key Questions
  • Foundation? Authority comes from God — since humans are social by nature and need someone directing all toward the common good.
  • Purpose? To guarantee ordered community life without crushing individual freedom — directing freedom toward the common good.
  • Limits? Must operate within morality and law. Citizens are conscience-bound to obey only when authority acts justly.
  • Subject? The PEOPLE hold sovereignty. They transfer it to elected representatives but retain the right to evaluate and replace them.
Authority as Moral Force — 3 Requirements
  1. Must be guided by moral law — not arbitrary power or political whims. The moral order exists in God; cut from God, it disintegrates.
  2. Must recognize, respect, and promote human values — dignity, rights, and truths that no state can create, change, or destroy.
  3. Must enact just laws — laws that match human dignity and right reason. An unjust law is not really a law — it is an act of violence.
Individual & National Rights

Individual Political Rights:

  • Participate freely in political process
  • Free speech, press, and dissent
  • Freedom of education
  • Security from arrest/torture for political views
  • Juridical protection of all rights

Rights of Every Nation:

  • Right to exist
  • Right to self-development
  • Right to the means of development
  • Right to lead its own development
  • Right to a good name
  • Duty to respect these rights in others
Fundamental Right All rights flow from one root: the right to life. From this flows the right to work, housing, healthcare, speech, education, and family.
When Can Citizens Disobey or Resist?
Right to Conscientious Objection Citizens are NOT obligated to follow civil laws that contradict moral order, fundamental human rights, or the Gospel. Refusing to cooperate with morally evil acts is both a moral duty and a basic human right.

Right to Resist is legitimate when natural law is seriously and repeatedly violated. Five criteria must be met:

  1. Certain, grave, and prolonged violation of fundamental rights
  2. All other means of redress have been exhausted
  3. Resistance will not provoke worse disorders
  4. Well-founded hope of success
  5. No better solution is reasonably foreseeable
Punishment, Solidarity & Charity

Punishment exists to protect the common good, correct offenders, and restore justice — not revenge. It must respect human dignity, allow religious access, and aim at re-insertion into society.

💙

Conclusion

Church + Government must work together. Government protects human persons with power and resources. The Church proclaims the Gospel and serves as the conscience of society. Solidarity and charity — loving neighbor as self — are the foundations of a just political life.
MODULE 03

Social Analysis

The See–Judge–Act method for understanding society
What Is Social Analysis?

"Social analysis can be defined as an effort to obtain a more complete picture of a social situation by exploring its historical and structural relationships."

— Joe Holland, S.J. & Peter Henriot, S.J.

Simply put: Social Analysis is looking at a social problem DEEPLY — not just at what's happening on the surface, but WHY it's happening, and what structures in society cause it.

The Framework: See – Judge – Act

👁 SEE

Reflect on your own situation. Look at resources, power, relationships, meaning. Gather facts and listen to people most affected.

⚖️ JUDGE

Evaluate what you see in light of Gospel values and Church teaching. What does faith say about this situation?

🚀 ACT

Decide on action. What concrete steps can be taken to address the root causes and bring about justice?
3 Steps of Doing Social Analysis
  • Make explicit your values and biases. No investigation is value-free. Acknowledge the perspectives and stances you bring to the task before you begin.
  • Describe the situation. Get an initial picture — gather facts and trends, hear stories, talk to people most directly involved, conduct surveys.
  • Analyze the situation. Work through: What is the HISTORY here? What STRUCTURES maintain this problem? Who holds POWER? What are the key RELATIONSHIPS? What is the likely FUTURE direction?
What Factors Cause Social Problems?
Cultural Factors Assumptions and values baked into society — shaped by education, media, religion. These determine what people think is "normal" or "acceptable."
Social Structures How society is divided into groups — families, social classes, ethnic groups. These determine who has power and who doesn't.
Critical Thinking Required Always ask: Who benefits from this situation? Who loses? Who has power? Who is powerless? These structural questions reveal root causes.
Types of Issues to Analyze

Economic Issues:

  • Labor and unemployment
  • Poverty and income distribution
  • Production and consumption patterns

Political Issues:

  • Government policies and justice systems
  • Trade unions and lobby groups
  • Graft and corruption

Other social issues: overseas workers, street children, child labor, urban poor, teenage pregnancy, bullying, suicide, sexual harassment, debt burden.

🔍

Analysis Process

1. Investigate how things ARE → 2. Ask WHY things are so → 3. Get behind the issue to find root causes
Key Analysis Questions
  • Are the causes of hunger/poverty linked to economic, political, cultural, or social structures?
  • Does anyone experience poverty or discrimination in your place today?
  • What factors (resources, power, relationships) contribute to this?
  • Who benefits from the current arrangement? Who bears the cost?
MODULE 04

Laudato Si'

"Praise Be to You" — Pope Francis on Care for Our Common Home
What Is Laudato Si'?

📖 Name

"Laudato Si'" means Praise Be to You in medieval Italian — from St. Francis' Canticle of the Creatures (13th c.)

📜 Document Type

A Papal Encyclical (formal Church letter/teaching) — the highest form of papal teaching document

👥 Audience

Written to ALL people — not just Catholics. Addressed to everyone, including non-religious people, about our shared home.
Chapter 1: Our Common Home Is in Crisis

The earth is a shared inheritance — not just for people alive today, but for past and future generations. And it is in serious trouble.

🏭 Pollution & Waste

Causes millions of premature deaths. The earth "looks like an immense pile of filth." Throwaway culture must end.

🌡️ Climate Change

Climate is a common good. Most global warming is caused by human activity (greenhouse gases). The poor are most affected.

💧 Water Crisis

Access to safe drinking water is a basic human right. Denying the poor water denies their dignity.

🦁 Biodiversity Loss

All creatures are connected. Extinction cannot be predicted or undone. Protecting biodiversity is essential.

🏙️ Society in Crisis

The current development model has not led to "integral development." Cities are wasteful and increasingly unlivable.

🌍 Global Inequality

The poor are "collateral damage" in global debates. A true ecological approach must be a social approach — hear BOTH the cry of the earth AND the cry of the poor.
Chapter 2: Gospel of Creation
Three Fundamental Relationships Human life is grounded in: (1) relationship with God, (2) relationship with our neighbor, (3) relationship with the earth. Sin breaks all three simultaneously.
  • The earth is a gift, not a possession — given to us to administer, not destroy
  • We must respect the laws of nature; all creation has its own goodness
  • Each creature has its own purpose — none is superfluous
  • The material universe speaks of God's love — it is the place of God's presence
  • The earth is "a shared inheritance whose fruits are meant to benefit everyone"
  • Owners must administer property with respect for its "social mortgage"
Chapter 3: Human Roots of the Ecological Crisis
Anthropocentrism The belief that humans are radically separate from and above nature. This devalues creation and leads to "practical relativism" — treating creation as only useful to humans.
The Technocratic Paradigm Accepting every advance in technology for profit, without concern for negative human impact. Finance "overwhelms the real economy" of human flourishing.
⚠️

Key Conclusion

"By itself the market cannot guarantee integral human development and social inclusion." Markets must be regulated when they fail the common good.
Chapter 4: Integral Ecology
Core Idea: Everything Is Connected Ecology covers not just nature but all dimensions of human life — academics, economics, health, governance, culture, daily life. You cannot fix the environment while ignoring poverty.
  • Care for creation is inseparable from the preferential option for the poor
  • Environmental justice must be understood as intergenerational — we owe it to future generations
  • Solidarity and commitment to the common good are ecological duties
Chapter 5: Action & Dialogue

Guided by the principle of subsidiarity — address challenges at the lowest possible but highest necessary level of society.

  • Fossil fuels must be progressively replaced without delay
  • Renewable energy subsidies should help developing countries access clean tech
  • "Common but differentiated responsibilities" — wealthier/more industrial nations bear greater responsibility for solutions
  • Apply the precautionary principle: if serious irreversible damage is possible, halt or modify even without absolute proof
  • Marginalized communities must be able to participate in environmental decisions
Chapter 6: Ecological Education & Spirituality
  • We need personal and communal conversion away from consumerism and collective selfishness
  • Ecological education must happen everywhere: schools, families, media, churches, political institutions
  • Consumer boycotts can successfully change how businesses operate
  • Sacraments and the Sabbath are essential to right relationship with creation
🌿

The 3 GSW Questions to Ask Yourself

How are we adding to the deterioration of the Earth? · What can we do to help care for our common home? · How can we inspire others to reconsider their lifestyle?
MODULE 05

Promotion of Peace

Peace as God's gift, humanity's project, and our shared responsibility
What Is Peace?
Peace Is More Than Absence of War Biblical "Shalom" (Hebrew) means completeness and fullness of life. Peace is not just a ceasefire — it is the fullness of justice, love, and right relationship with God, neighbor, and creation.

☮️ Peace = God's Attribute

"The Lord is peace" (Judges 6:24). Peace is a primary attribute of God himself — not a human invention.

🕊️ Peace = God's Gift

Far from being a human achievement alone, peace is one of the greatest gifts God offers to humanity.

⚖️ Peace = Human Duty

Peace is a universal duty — a value every person and community is responsible for building and protecting.
Biblical Foundations of Peace
  • Old Testament: Peace produces fruitfulness, well-being, prosperity, absence of fear, and profound joy. The Messiah is called "Prince of Peace" (Is 9:5).
  • Jesus: "Peace I leave with you; my peace I give to you — not as the world gives" (John 14:27). After resurrection, His greeting was always: "Peace be with you."
  • Peace = Reconciliation: First with God (through Christ), then with others. The Our Father links forgiveness from God to our forgiveness of others.
  • Beatitudes: "Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called children of God" (Mt 5:9).
Peace: Fruit of Justice AND Love
Peace as Fruit of Justice Peace is threatened when people are not given what they are due as human persons. Defending and promoting human rights is ESSENTIAL to building peace.
Peace as Fruit of Love "True and lasting peace is more a matter of love than of justice." Justice removes obstacles; love actually creates peace itself.

"Violence is evil, that violence is unacceptable as a solution to problems, that violence is unworthy of man. Violence is a lie, for it goes against the truth of our faith, the truth of our humanity."

The Failure of Peace: War
War Is Always a Defeat War is described as a "scourge," an "unnecessary massacre," and "the failure of all true humanism." It creates new and more complicated conflicts. "Nothing is lost by peace; everything may be lost by war."

War is never an appropriate way to resolve problems between nations — not in the past, not now, not ever. The damage is not only material but also moral.

When Is Defense Justified? (Just War Doctrine)

A war of aggression is intrinsically immoral. But legitimate defense may be justified. Four strict conditions must ALL be met:

  1. Damage from the aggressor is lasting, grave, and certain
  2. All other means have been shown to be ineffective
  3. There are serious prospects of success
  4. The use of arms will not produce evils graver than the original evil
🛡️

Important Distinctions

Self-defense ≠ domination. Possessing weapons does not justify using them for political goals. Military personnel must REFUSE orders that violate human rights — they cannot hide behind "just following orders."
Building Peace — Practical Principles
  • Peace must begin in the heart of every person, then spread to families, communities, and nations
  • The root causes of conflict — injustice, poverty, exploitation — must be actively removed
  • "Another name for peace is development" — a collective responsibility
  • International organizations (like the UN) are needed to resolve conflicts peacefully
  • Conscientious objectors who refuse military service must be offered alternative community service
  • Innocent civilians must be protected — humanitarian aid cannot be weaponized
MODULE 06

Promotion of Active Non-Violence

Not passive silence, but courageous, creative resistance to evil
What Is Active Non-Violence?

Active Non-Violence is NOT passivity. It is a powerful, deliberate, courageous way of confronting injustice without resorting to physical violence. It uses truth, dialogue, moral force, and solidarity as its weapons.

Compendium §496 — The Foundation Violence is rejected as a solution. It is "a lie" that destroys human dignity. But the Church calls for unarmed prophets — people who defend human rights using nonviolent means available to the weakest, without harming others' rights.
Key Documents & Sources

📗 Compendium (2004)

§496: Rejects violence as a "lie" against human dignity
§515: Address root causes of violence — injustice and human rights violations
§517: Reconciliation and forgiveness are essential for lasting peace

✉️ Pope Francis (2017)

Nonviolence: A Style of Politics for Peace — His World Day of Peace message calling nonviolence a political method for our time, not just personal virtue

📘 Fratelli Tutti (2020)

"Culture of encounter." Firm commitment to nonviolence in a fractured world. Challenges modern war (§256-262), advocates dialogue, rejects the death penalty and vengeance

📕 Christus Vivit (2019)

Urges YOUNG PEOPLE to embrace active nonviolence, social friendship, and faith-based commitment to peace and justice. §168-170: proactive, creative engagement in building a new society
Core Principles of Active Non-Violence
  1. Reject violence as a solution. Violence is intrinsically wrong — it destroys what it claims to defend: dignity, life, and freedom.
  2. Address root causes. Injustice, poverty, and violations of rights are the breeding ground of violence. Fix these and violence loses its fuel (§515).
  3. Pursue reconciliation and forgiveness. No lasting peace without healing. Forgiveness is not weakness — it is the path to genuine peace (§517).
  4. Foster "social friendship." Counter "social enmity" by building bonds across difference — the opposite of division and hatred.
  5. Dialogue over conflict. Always exhaust every avenue for dialogue before any other option. "Meeting and negotiating" reveals our shared humanity.
  6. Dignity of every person. Reject the death penalty and vengeance as they deny the inherent dignity God gave every human being.
For Young People — Christus Vivit §168-170
Pope Francis to the Youth Don't be inward-looking or selfish. Be proactively, creatively engaged in building a new, more just society. "Social friendship" — real bonds between different people — is what defeats the "social enmity" that drives violence and hatred.

🤝 Social Friendship

Build genuine relationships across divisions of class, ethnicity, politics. Counter polarization with encounter.

🕊️ Creative Engagement

Find new, nonviolent ways to address injustice — art, advocacy, community, dialogue, witness.

✝️ Faith-Based Commitment

Root peace work in faith — not as an abstract ideal but as a concrete, daily practice of love and justice.
Non-Violence vs. Violence — Quick Contrast

❌ Violence...

  • Is a lie against truth
  • Destroys what it claims to defend
  • Creates new and worse conflicts
  • Is unworthy of human dignity
  • Seeks power through domination

✅ Active Non-Violence...

  • Speaks truth to power
  • Defends dignity without destroying it
  • Builds lasting, reconciled peace
  • Reflects evangelical charity
  • Seeks justice through love and truth
✨

The Final Word

Active non-violence is a style of politics and a way of life — not retreat or passivity. It is the most courageous and most Christian response to a violent world. It trusts that truth, love, and human dignity are stronger than any weapon.
